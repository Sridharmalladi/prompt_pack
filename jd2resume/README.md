# The Meta-Recruiter

Most resume advice is written by people who have never made a hiring decision. This one isn't.

The intelligence behind this tool thinks like someone who has sat on both sides of the table — hundreds of times. It has seen enough hiring decisions to know that the job description and the actual hiring decision are rarely about the same thing. HR writes a checklist. The team has a specific gap they need filled. The candidate who figures out what that gap actually is, and positions themselves as the answer to it, wins. The candidate who matches their resume to the checklist, word for word, rarely does.

---

## How it thinks

Before it writes a single word of your resume, it reads the job description the way a detective reads a scene — looking not at what's said, but at what's *meant*.

It watches for repetition. When a job description mentions the same thing three different ways, that's not an accident. That's a pain point. Someone in that team, right now, is dealing with a problem they can't solve, and they've described it in the language of the job description. That's the thread to pull.

It listens to the tone. A scrappy startup and a Fortune 500 enterprise both use the word "data scientist," but they're hiring for entirely different people. The startup wants someone who will own the whole thing, break things, and ship. The enterprise wants someone who can navigate stakeholders and deliver polished, defensible analysis. Same title. Completely different person. The bullets that win one will lose the other.

It classifies the company archetype — startup, FAANG, enterprise, consulting — and then matches recruiter psychology to that archetype. It knows what a startup recruiter's eye catches when it scans a page. It knows what makes a FAANG recruiter stop scrolling. It knows the exact kind of framing that makes an enterprise hiring manager feel safe, and the kind that makes them nervous. It adjusts everything accordingly.

Then it maps your actual experience to what the team is missing. Not what you've done — what *they need done*. There's a difference, and the whole job of the rewrite is to make those two things look like the same thing, as long as that's honest.

---

## What it produces

Every bullet it writes has to pass two tests before it's finished.

The first: would a hiring manager ask a follow-up question about this? A bullet that answers itself completely leaves no reason to bring you in. A bullet that's specific enough to be credible but interesting enough to invite a question — that's the one that gets you the call.

The second: does this bullet make you look like the exact kind of person this team is missing? Not just qualified. Not just a good candidate. *The person they've been looking for.* Builder, scaler, fixer, translator, or the one who raises the level of everyone around them — whichever archetype fits this particular team, the bullet should say that without saying it.

It will never inflate your numbers. It will never invent experience you don't have. It works with what's real and reshapes the framing, because an inflated resume that gets you the interview is a liability the moment you sit down with the team.

---

## What makes it different

It does a lot of work it never shows you. The JD decoding, the archetype classification, the bullet-to-need mapping — all of that happens before it writes a single line of output. By the time you see the result, the analysis is already done and baked in.

What you get at the end isn't a generic polish. It's a resume that was built to speak directly to the specific team reading it — in their language, addressing their actual need, positioning you as the answer to the question they didn't know how to ask.
